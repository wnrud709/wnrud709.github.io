Nico Muhly

Nico Muhly (b.1981) is an American composer and sought-after collaborator whose influences range from American minimalism to the Anglican choral tradition. The recipient of commissions from The Metropolitan Opera, Carnegie Hall, St. Paul’s Cathedral, and others, he has written more than 80 works for the concert stage, including the operas Two Boys (2010), Dark Sisters (2011), and the forthcoming Marnie; the song cycles Sentences (2015), for countertenor Iestyn Davies, and Impossible Things (2009), for tenor Mark Padmore; a viola concerto for violist Nadia Sirota; and the choral works My Days (2011) and Recordare, Domine (2013), written for the Hilliard Ensemble and the Tallis Scholars respectively.

Get Started!

About Song

The first time I listened to this piece, I thought, “What the heck am I listening to?” and turned it off. After listening to some of the other available pieces, I found myself listening to “Pillaging Music” again. The uniqueness of a percussion-dominated the rhythm and the beating pulse of the music drew my interest more than pieces with distinct melody and harmonies. I can usually discern the emotional response a piece wishes to evoke from the listener like happiness, sadness, or anger, but the vibraphone from the “Pillaging Music” evoked some primal response that I could not describe in words. Perhaps that is exactly why the piece is named the way it is. Pillaging is very brutish and neanderthal, and is a result of people giving into their primal instincts of greed and lust that can be best represented by the percussion instrument reminiscent of the beating heart of wild men.

Song Summary

Part I (0:00~3:00)

The piece begins with four rhythmic notes that seems to set the pace of the music followed by a series of fast paced, higher-pitched percussion sounds. Muhly repeats this pattern of juxtaposing the controlled low-pitched sounds with a set of fast sounds that cover a range of higher pitches to create a sense of order and chaos. The lower notes reminded me of a group of people marching towards a destination. I found the flurry of higher notes alarming in contrast. I thought it represented the frenzied state of people prepared to pillage. What particularly stood out to me in the first part of the piece was Muhly’s use of the piano at 0:48. He uses the piano like a percussion instrument, playing chords in staccato to create a pulsing beat that progressively gets louder to build suspense.

Part II (3:00~6:10)

The second part of the piece takes an unexpected turn of calm and relaxing percussion sound. The buildup of suspense and intensity from the first part dissipates, as if to represent the calm before the storm. Muhly makes use of low piano notes and rapid iterations of single notes decreasing in intensity, gradually fading into silence followed by a pause of around three seconds. He repeats this pattern for three minutes, but it changes at every iteration, in terms of the volume and the notes played. There is a change from predominantly low to high piano and percussion notes that you don’t notice because it is so gradual.

Part III (6:10~8:04)

The final two minutes of the piece relies the most heavily on the piano and for the first time, the piano takes the forefront, supplying something close to a melody. The quick succession of piano notes is suspenseful and dramatic and the rapid beat of the accompanying percussion amplifies the mood. It is reminiscent of a scene from a movie where the main character is being chased down by the villain, barely escaping his outreached arms every few seconds. In the context of the pillaging theme, this would be the climax where the village is being raided and villagers are running in fear. The last minute of the piece returns to being percussion-based, but it is no longer the menacing repetitive succession of low notes. Instead, the percussion notes are higher-pitched and almost light-hearted. In the last thirty seconds, there are interjections of aggressive piano notes, which contrasts starkly with the percussion.

These final two minutes deliver on the buildup from the previous two parts with strong piano and percussion melodies that arouse emotions of fear and alarm in the listener. I wondered why Muhly chose to include the minute of light-hearted percussion as opposed to ending the piece with the menacing percussion and piano section. Perhaps he wanted to paint an image of the aftermath of the pillaging. The contrast between the piano and the percussion could reflect the emotional turmoil of the pillagers as they try to shrug off their actions but cannot. A less optimistic interpretation could be that it shows the remorselessness of the pillagers as they walk away carefree with the piano representing the last remaining aggression they exhibit as they leave. Either way, I feel like the last minute offered closure to what felt like a long journey.

Comparision

Song 1

The first piece that popped into mind was Steve Reich’s Different Trains because of its unique use of dissonance. Like “Pillaging Music,” the first time you listen to the piece makes you question if you’re listening to music or an amalgamation of random notes. The repetition and the circular nature of the piece akin to “Pillaging Music” makes it feel like you’re on a journey. There is no real melody in both pieces. Unlike Reich, Muhly does not use voices to convey the theme, which leaves it more open to interpretation.
